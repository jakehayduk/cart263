The Creative Act, A Way of Being by Rick Rubin talks about the experimentation stage of the artistic development process the author uses the analogy of seeds as potential starting points he talks about them as if they can be nurtured, each idea can grow if you find the right way to cultivate it the seeds will look and grow different depending on the artist and every seed this is the phase of experimentation, where progressing quickly doesn't matter nearly as much as revealing the different iterations (seeds) that have the most promise we don't want to extinguish even the smallest flame, if there is a chance it can grow into a wildfire don't be tempted to steer the development of a seed toward a specific goal or preconceived idea, because then its possibilities will be limited. "Not every seed must grow. But it may be there is a right time for each one." they are saying that even if there's not much development in each idea, hold on to them rather than throwing them away because because the time for you to engage with it depends on many external and internal factors of your life, and the time that it speaks to you most can change. I also think that even if you plant a seed, it doesn't mean you have to be the one to keep watering it. If there's a right time for them, there could also be a 'right' person that can better maintain and continue to help it grow. So even if you start one, maybe it's not for you. an example I have is when I wrote a song and then my friend helped me finish it, so it grew into also becoming his, and only then could I consider it a finalized work Failure I think that failure is a powerful artistic tool. Failure is really just the reminder of all the ways we tried to prod and poke our plants into submission. Trying to make them grow forcefully. That can seem discouraging on its own. But let me remind you of the many seeds we've left unattended, the ones that have stopped growing. Those are not failures of artistry, but rather possibilities that have yet to be fully explored. Failure as an artist is limiting those possibilities in this stage of development and ignoring your feelings of interest in certain directions, failing to surprise yourself. Let yourself serve the work, and it will be a great service to yourself. I believe that every seed has a sort of potential energy. It will naturally want to grow one way, and it's best not to disrupt it. Many things have been discovered by accident in the experimentation phase and there are many more surprising discoveries waiting for us. How do we know when an idea is flourishing? Does anyone think it's possible for someone to have no seeds?
